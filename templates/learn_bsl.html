British Sign Language

BSL is the sign language of the Deaf community in the United Kingdom. Unlike ASL, BSL fingerspelling uses two hands, and its grammar and vocabulary are entirely different from English and ASL.

Two-Handed Fingerspelling

Each letter is formed using both hands together, rather than a single handshape — one hand often acts as a "base" while the other traces or points to it.

Fingerspelling tips Keep both hands visible and at a steady, comfortable height — since BSL fingerspelling needs two hands working together, it's easy to lose clarity if one hand drops out of view. As with any fingerspelling, favor accuracy over speed while you're still learning.

Numbers 0–10

BSL numbers have their own distinct handshapes, separate from fingerspelling — and some regions of the UK sign numbers slightly differently.

Worth knowing Like ASL, BSL generally signs the item before the quantity to show a plural, and doesn't use a separate word ending the way English uses "-s". BSL grammar follows its own topic-comment word order, which often differs from English sentence structure.

Deaf Culture, in Brief

BSL is a complete, independent language — not a signed version of English — with its own grammar, facial grammar (raised eyebrows for questions, for example), and regional variation across the UK. As with ASL, Deaf (capitalized) refers to people who share Deaf language and culture, while deaf (lowercase) describes audiological hearing loss. Practicing with fluent and Deaf signers is the fastest way to build real fluency.

Good to know BSL, ASL, and other sign languages are not mutually intelligible — a signer fluent in one usually can't automatically understand the other, just like spoken English and French. Once you're comfortable, try the For You tools and sign to the camera in real time.
