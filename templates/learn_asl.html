American Sign Language

ASL is a full, independent language with its own grammar — used mainly across the United States and English-speaking Canada. Start with the alphabet, then numbers, then everyday phrases.

Fingerspelling Alphabet

Every letter has its own one-handed shape. Fingerspelling is used for names, places, and any word that doesn't have its own dedicated sign.

{% for letter in "ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ" %}
{{ letter }}
Letter {{ letter }}
{% endfor %}
Fingerspelling tips Go for clarity over speed — it's fine to be slow while you're learning. As you spell, silently shape the word's sounds with your mouth rather than naming each letter, and pause briefly between separate words so they don't blur together.

Numbers 0–10

Numbers in ASL each get their own handshape, separate from the alphabet.

{% for n in range(0, 11) %}
{{ n }}
Number {{ n }}
{% endfor %}
Two things worth knowing Numbers 1–5 are typically signed palm-in, while 6–10 flip to palm-out. And to show a plural, ASL signs the item first and the quantity second (e.g. BOOK then TWO) — there's no separate "-s" ending like in English.

Deaf Culture, in Brief

ASL isn't just a manual code for English — it's a full language with its own grammar, carried by Deaf culture and history. A useful convention: Deaf (capitalized) refers to people who share Deaf language and culture, while deaf (lowercase) simply describes audiological hearing loss. When you're learning, practicing with fluent and Deaf signers — not just a screen — is the fastest way to actually get good.
